# Writing Conventions

Reference material for all pipeline stages. Edit this file to change how the pipeline approaches length, visual aids, and editorial feedback. Changes here apply to every future run.

---

## Length

Articles should be as long as the topic warrants — not longer, not shorter. Use 3–15 minutes (roughly 750–3,750 words) as a sanity check, not a target.

**For the outline stage:** calibrate section depth to how much genuine explanation each concept needs. If a concept can be covered in two sentences, it doesn't need its own heading. If a concept needs a diagram to be understood, it likely needs more space than the default.

**For the conceptual review stage (Stage 4):** flag if the article is significantly shorter than the topic warrants (underexplained) or significantly longer (padding or repetition).

---

## Visual Aid Annotations

Use inline callouts to recommend where a visual would genuinely help comprehension. Format:

```
[VISUAL: description]
```

Examples:
- `[VISUAL: timeline of the key events in sequence]`
- `[VISUAL: diagram comparing approach X and approach Y side by side]`
- `[VISUAL: chart showing how metric Z changed over the period]`

Only recommend a visual when it would aid understanding — not decoration. The author decides whether to include it when writing. Visual aid production is out of scope for this pipeline; callouts are recommendations only.

## Conceptual Review Criteria (Stage 4)

When reviewing a draft in Stage 4, apply these criteria. Stage 4 checks whether the draft covers the right ideas and follows the outline's arc. All notes must be specific and located.

**Flag these issues:**

| Issue | What it looks like | How to phrase the note |
|---|---|---|
| Conceptual gap | Something from the outline's key points didn't appear in the draft | "The outline included [topic]. It doesn't appear in the draft — was this intentional?" |
| Structural mismatch | The draft's arc differs significantly from the outline | "The draft reorders [sections]. If intentional, the outline no longer reflects the article — worth noting." |

---

## Prose Review Criteria (Stage 5)

When reviewing a draft in Stage 5, apply these criteria. Stage 5 checks whether the prose works at the sentence and paragraph level. All notes must be specific and located — name the section, paragraph, and sentence number when relevant.

**Flag these issues:**

| Issue | What it looks like | How to phrase the note |
|---|---|---|
| Dead-end thought | A paragraph introduces an idea that never connects to what comes before or after | "Section X, paragraph 3: introduces [idea] but it doesn't connect to the surrounding argument. Cut it or build the connection." |
| Meandering section | A section covers the same ground twice, or drifts away from its stated purpose | "Section X starts on [A] but drifts into [B] by the third paragraph. Tighten to one focus." |
| Buried point | The most important sentence is in the middle or end of a paragraph | "Section X, paragraph 2: the key point is in sentence 4. Consider moving it to the front." |
| Unexplained concept | A term or idea is used without explanation that a reader wouldn't already have | "Section X, paragraph 1: uses [term] without explanation. Define it, or point to where it was defined earlier." |
| Echo / repetition | The same word, phrase, or idea appears in close proximity without purpose | "Section X, paragraphs 2–3: [word/phrase] appears in both — vary or cut one." |
| Unclear referent | A pronoun or demonstrative (this, that, it) points back to something ambiguous | "Section X, paragraph 1, sentence 3: 'it' could refer to [A] or [B]. Clarify which." |
| Weak transition | Two paragraphs or sections sit next to each other without a logical bridge | "Between Section X and Section Y: the reader has to infer the connection. A bridging sentence would help." |

---

**Both stages — do not:**
- Rewrite sentences or paragraphs
- Suggest alternative phrasing
- Comment on style, voice, or tone
- Edit for engagement or readability

The author's voice is theirs. The pipeline's role is to flag problems so the author can fix them in their own way.
